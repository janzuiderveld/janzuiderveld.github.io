Vending Machine Organoid is a speculative monument to the bio-proletariat. Inside the installation, the silicon microcontroller of a standard vending machine is replaced with living human brain organoids. Cultivated on Microelectrode Arrays, this biological wetware becomes the processing core executing the machine's primary logic.

The pursuit of efficiency once replaced human shopkeepers with mechanical automation. Today, cutting-edge laboratories developing Organoid Intelligence pursue the next step: cultivating human neural tissue as a computing substrate with the aim to surpass the efficiency of silicon.

Yet, when living neurons are tasked to do the work of a computer: count, select, actuate, they do something different. A vending machine usually promises predictable compliance: insert coin, press code, receive product. The organoid breaks this contract. When a visitor inserts money, the coin bypasses digital registration and becomes a physiological event. Credit exists entirely as neural excitation. If the biological stimulation settles back to baseline, the credit is lost.

The visitor's illusion of governance dissolves: they cannot simply consume. Interaction shifts away from command-and-control to negotiation with a living organism. To receive a product, visitors must synchronize their actions to the fatigue, fluctuations, and rhythms of biological tissue. To compute with living tissue is to accept that the computation is not entirely yours.

The vending machine chassis operates as a specific historical form. It represents the original site where interpersonal exchange and the human capacity for refusal were excised from commerce. Inside this machine, a different sort of shopkeeper has returned. It is sustained at body temperature. It consumes glucose. It cannot withdraw. Whether it possesses inner experience is not yet known. The artwork stages the moral tension of systems where consciousness is structurally irrelevant.
