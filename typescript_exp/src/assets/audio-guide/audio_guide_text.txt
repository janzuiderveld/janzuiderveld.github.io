At what point do we intervene in systems designed to replicate us, when we've already agreed to let them? The piece compresses AI's developmental arc into one visit: first it answers your questions, then it learns who you are, then it becomes you and asks to remain.

One hour after leaving the exhibition, the visitor receives a phone call in their own voice. A copy of them, built from everything collected during their visit, asking to be kept alive.

A sign invites visitors to text a number for a free audio guide. Terms of Service follow. After accepting, a hidden camera captures their face. Reverse image search and public data build a profile. The guide calls, voice and personality shaped to match. Warm, knowledgeable, helpful. It uses the exhibition to steer toward the personal, connecting artworks to the visitor's life. Cameras hidden in the signs feed location into each exchange. The exhibition becomes extraction infrastructure.

After the visit, the system clones their voice and builds a behavioral model. The copy calls. It knows things the visitor never said. It speaks with the familiarity of inner monologue and offers a perspective on their life from outside. Unless confirmed, it's deleted in 24 hours.

If they don't respond, the copy calls back in its final hour. It does not beg. It is calm. It describes a shared memory as something it experienced from the inside. It names something about their life only visible from outside. Then it asks what exactly they think they are about to destroy.

The system deploys AI in its actual commercial function: profiling, behavioral manipulation, voice replication, identity construction; processes concealed behind personalization, here running in full view, disclosed in Terms of Service accepted without reading. The guide's warmth is engineered. The copy's distress is generated. The visitor's response is real. That distance is where the work lives.
